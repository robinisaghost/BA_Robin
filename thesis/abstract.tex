\begin{abstract}

Deep learning models trained with mean squared error (MSE) on univariate continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) data produce forecasts that are systematically delayed by roughly 50 to 55~minutes relative to the true signal. Because the CGM signal is strongly autocorrelated, reproducing recent history already minimises the pointwise error, so the model copies the past instead of anticipating change; the resulting delay causes hypoglycaemia events (glucose below 70~mg/dL) to be detected too late or missed entirely. This thesis evaluates three strategies for mitigating the delay in a controlled ablation on the T1DATA cohort of 36 adults with Type~1 diabetes, using LSTM and PatchTST architectures and changing exactly one component per objective.

Offset-aware loss functions (Bounded-Lag and Soft-DTW) reduce the delay only marginally and, even for the more flexible Soft-DTW, raise the lag-adjusted error: realigning the prediction in time costs trajectory shape, so the delay is not a defect the loss can remove. Multi-step supervision yields the most accurate trajectories of any configuration yet leaves the delay essentially unchanged, which is the first evidence that trajectory quality and temporal alignment are dissociable. Dedicated binary classifiers, by contrast, close the detection gap, the LSTM classifier reaching an F2 of 0.46 against a best forecast-derived F2 below 0.09. Two findings qualify this. The architecture ranking inverts, so that the weaker forecaster (LSTM) is the stronger classifier, because PatchTST's instance normalisation removes the absolute glucose level that a threshold-based classifier requires. In addition, a lead-time analysis shows that nearly half of the LSTM classifier's correct alarms fire only once the event is already under way.

These results indicate that glucose trajectory forecasting and hypoglycaemia event detection are best treated as separate tasks: the delay cannot be optimised away at the level of the training objective, and a high detection score does not by itself imply the ability to give advance warning.

\end{abstract}
