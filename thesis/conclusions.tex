\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusions}

This chapter summarises the central empirical findings of the ablation study, discusses the underlying methodological limitations, and outlines strategic directions for subsequent research. Section~\ref{sec:conc:summary} aggregates the core insights from all three research objectives. Section~\ref{sec:conc:limitations} assesses the structural boundaries of the evaluated framework, and Section~\ref{sec:conc:futurework} identifies directions for future investigative work.

% ================================================================
\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:conc:summary}
% ================================================================

This study set out to determine whether the systematic prediction delay in MSE-trained CGM forecasting can be mitigated, and whether such mitigation translates into clinically useful hypoglycaemia detection. Across the three controlled objectives the evidence converges on one conclusion: the delay is a structural property of the forecasting setup rather than a tunable artefact, and trajectory forecasting and event detection are best treated as separate problems.

The delay is structural. Under MSE training, both architectures displace their predictions by roughly 50 to 55~minutes, the temporal signature of a model that minimises pointwise error by reproducing recent history on a signal whose lag-1 autocorrelation exceeds 0.98 (Section~\ref{sec:intro:timeshift}). This is not a defect of a particular architecture: the LSTM and PatchTST baselines exhibit the same lag despite their entirely different inductive biases, which identifies the loss--signal interaction, not model capacity, as the root cause.

Objective~1 (offset-aware losses) does not resolve the delay. Bounded-Lag and Soft-DTW reduce the mean lag only marginally (at most 5.2~minutes), and in every architecture--loss combination RMSE$^*$ rises above the MSE baseline (Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:obj1}). The interpretation is decisive: tolerating timing errors in the loss does not teach the model to anticipate change; it merely licenses a shifted, shape-degraded prediction. Event-detection gains are correspondingly negligible, with all forecast-derived F2 scores remaining below 0.09.

Objective~2 (multi-step supervision) yields the most accurate trajectories but does not change the picture for detection. Multi-step MSE achieves the best RMSE$^*$ among the LSTM configurations (20.74~mg/dL); for PatchTST the single-step baseline remains marginally best (14.21 vs.\ 14.53~mg/dL), so broader supervision does not consistently help the attention-based model. Crucially, the mean lag falls by at most about one minute and F2 stays below 0.09. Better trajectories therefore do not imply better detection, the first concrete sign that the two objectives are dissociable.

Objective~3 (direct classification) closes the detection gap and yields the central finding. A dedicated LSTM classifier reaches F2~=~0.463 and recall~=~0.693, against a best forecast-derived F2 of 0.013, a large improvement that is statistically significant after Holm--Bonferroni correction (Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:significance}). The result reverses the forecasting ranking: the LSTM, the weaker forecaster by RMSE$^*$, is the stronger classifier, whereas PatchTST's RevIN, beneficial for forecasting, removes the absolute glucose level a threshold-based classifier requires and caps it at F2~=~0.178 (Section~\ref{sec:bg:models:patchtst}).

F2 alone, however, overstates the clinical value. The lead-time analysis (Section~\ref{sec:exp:obj3:leadtime}) shows that 49.5\% of the LSTM classifier's true-positive alarms fire at zero lead time: close to half of its alarms confirm a hypoglycaemic episode that has already begun rather than anticipate one. The PatchTST classifier, though weaker overall, issues a larger share of genuine advance warnings, with a median lead time of 15~minutes against 5~minutes for the LSTM. High recall and reliable anticipation are thus distinct properties that the F2 score does not separate.

Taken together, the results support three conclusions. First, the prediction delay cannot be optimised away at the loss level; it is inherent to pointwise regression on a highly autocorrelated signal. Second, forecasting quality and detection quality are achieved by different configurations and do not coincide, so the two tasks should be addressed independently. Third, the strongest practical design evaluated here pairs a multi-step MSE forecaster for trajectory quality with a dedicated LSTM binary classifier for detection support; as the lead-time and precision results show, that classifier functions mainly as a confirmatory rather than an anticipatory detector and would require false-alarm mitigation before clinical use.

\section{Methodological Limitations}
\label{sec:conc:limitations}
% ================================================================

Several structural constraints bound the conclusions of this study. First, all models operate exclusively on the univariate glucose signal and have no access to exogenous causal inputs such as insulin administration records or carbohydrate intake logs. Rapid glucose changes caused by a recent bolus dose or a meal cannot be anticipated from the glucose signal alone, which places a fundamental ceiling on predictive performance regardless of the model architecture or training objective employed.

Second, the lead-time analysis reveals that the LSTM classifier, despite achieving the highest F2, fires reactively in nearly half of all true-positive detections. A clinical alarm issued at the moment of hypoglycaemia onset provides limited actionable advance warning; the practical utility of this configuration for a patient who needs time to respond is therefore more restricted than the F2 score alone indicates.

Third, the event detection task is severely class-imbalanced, with only 5.7\% of labelled windows containing a positive event. This imbalance restricts achievable precision across all configurations and means that even a classifier with recall of 0.693 produces a large volume of false alarms, a factor that would require dedicated mitigation before any clinical deployment.

Finally, hyperparameters for both architectures were tuned on a single designated patient (ID~85202) rather than the full cohort, for computational reasons. The resulting configuration may not be optimal for all 36 patients, and this pragmatic compromise limits the generalisability of architecture-specific conclusions drawn from the study.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:conc:futurework}
% ================================================================

Several directions follow directly from the limitations identified above. The most consequential extension would be the integration of multimodal inputs, specifically logged insulin doses and carbohydrate records, to eliminate the information deficit that bounds the univariate approach. Such inputs would allow a model to anticipate glucose excursions driven by exogenous factors that are entirely invisible to a glucose-only model, and could substantially reduce the number of reactive alarms.

Within the offset-aware loss framework, evaluating wider Bounded-Lag tolerance windows (extending towards $D = 12$ steps to fully encompass the observed 50--55 minute systematic delay) may produce more substantial lag reductions than the $D = 3$ setting used here. Whether such extended windows still permit the model to learn meaningful temporal structure, rather than simply tolerating arbitrarily large offsets, warrants empirical investigation.

The class imbalance problem could be addressed through two complementary strategies: replacing the weighted binary cross-entropy with a focal loss formulation that down-weights easy negative examples during training, and augmenting the positive class through oversampling or synthetic data generation. Either approach may improve the precision side of the F2 score without sacrificing the recall gains achieved by the current classifiers.

Finally, a leave-one-out or cross-validated evaluation protocol would provide a more robust assessment of generalisation across the cohort than the single-patient hyperparameter tuning used in this study, and would enable a rigorous comparison of per-patient variability in model performance.
