\chapter{Experiments} \label{ch:experiments}
This chapter presents the empirical results of all experimental configurations evaluated across the cohort of 36 patients. Section~\ref{sec:exp:baseline} establishes the baseline performance under standard MSE training. Sections~\ref{sec:exp:obj1} through~\ref{sec:exp:obj3} report the results for each of the three research objectives. Section~\ref{sec:exp:overall} provides a comprehensive comparative summary across all ten model configurations.

\section{Baseline: Single-Step Forecasting} \label{sec:exp:baseline}
% ================================================================

Under standard MSE training, both architectures exhibit the systematic prediction delay that motivates this study. Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:baseline} reports the cohort-mean baseline results. The LSTM achieves an RMSE of 36.09~mg/dL with a mean lag of 51.2~minutes, while PatchTST achieves an RMSE of 39.27~mg/dL with a mean lag of 55.3~minutes. Both values indicate that the predicted trajectory is displaced forward by approximately ten steps relative to the true signal, which is consistent with the high lag-1 autocorrelation of the glucose series described in Section~\ref{sec:method:setup:cgm}. Fig.~\ref{fig:exp:baseline_curves} illustrates this delay for a representative patient segment (patient 85102, which is distinct from the Optuna tuning patient 85202; Section~\ref{sec:method:setup:splits}); Fig.~\ref{fig:exp:lag_dist} confirms that the delay is systematic and present in nearly all 36 patients for both architectures. The lag-adjusted RMSE (RMSE$^*$) of 21.65~mg/dL for LSTM and 14.21~mg/dL for PatchTST reveals that, once the systematic temporal displacement is corrected, both baselines produce comparatively accurate predictions. The gap between RMSE and RMSE$^*$ (14.4~mg/dL for LSTM, 25.1~mg/dL for PatchTST) quantifies the portion of raw prediction error that is removable by optimal temporal alignment, and is notably larger for PatchTST despite its similar lag in minutes.

\section{Objective 1: Offset-Aware Loss Functions} \label{sec:exp:obj1}
% ================================================================

This section evaluates whether replacing MSE with an offset-aware loss function reduces the systematic prediction delay. The Bounded-Lag loss and Soft-DTW are assessed separately; a joint interpretation is provided at the end of this section.

\subsection{Bounded-Lag Loss Results}

The Bounded-Lag loss (Section~\ref{sec:bg:losses:bl}) accepts a predicted value as correct if it falls within a fixed local neighbourhood of the true value, thereby removing the gradient penalty for small temporal offsets. As shown in Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:obj1}, the effect on the prediction delay is modest. For the LSTM, the mean lag decreases from 51.2 to 48.6~minutes, a reduction of 2.6~minutes, while RMSE remains nearly unchanged at 36.14~mg/dL. For PatchTST, the lag decreases by only 0.7~minutes (55.3 to 54.6~min) and RMSE stays at 39.30~mg/dL. Event detection improves marginally: the LSTM F2 increases from 0.004 to 0.013 and PatchTST F2 from 0.074 to 0.080. These results indicate that a locally tolerant loss reduces the lag metric slightly but is insufficient to overcome the broader structural delay in the predictions.

\subsection{Soft-DTW Loss Results}

The Soft-DTW loss~\citep{cuturi2017softdtw} (Section~\ref{sec:bg:losses:sdtw}) aligns two sequences by elastically warping the time axis to match their shapes. This allows one-to-many correspondences between time steps, which reduces the gradient penalty for temporally displaced predictions more aggressively than the Bounded-Lag loss. The results in Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:obj1} and Fig.~\ref{fig:exp:obj1_predictions} reveal a clear difference between the two architectures.

For the LSTM, Soft-DTW produces the largest lag reduction of all evaluated strategies: the mean lag decreases from 51.2 to 46.0~minutes, a reduction of 5.2~minutes. However, this comes at a cost: RMSE increases from 36.09 to 37.23~mg/dL (+3.2\%) and MAE increases from 27.12 to 28.76~mg/dL. Event detection does not improve; the F2 score of 0.010 is similar to the MSE baseline (0.004), and recall decreases from 0.004 to 0.009. The elastic alignment during training shifts the prediction closer in time to the true trajectory, but this does not translate into a meaningful gain in detecting threshold crossings.

For PatchTST, the behaviour is different. The lag decreases by only 0.9~minutes (55.3 to 54.4~min) while RMSE increases substantially from 39.27 to 43.24~mg/dL (+10.1\%). This amplitude degradation is attributable to the Reversible Instance Normalisation~\citep{kim2022revin} in PatchTST (Section~\ref{sec:bg:models:patchtst}): because Soft-DTW aligns shapes on the RevIN-normalised sequences, the resulting error signal is inconsistent with the restored absolute scale, producing large, incoherent gradient updates that degrade amplitude accuracy without reducing temporal displacement. A further consequence of RevIN for threshold-based classification is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:exp:obj3}.

The offset-aware losses achieve a partial temporal realignment at the cost of prediction shape accuracy, suggesting that tolerating timing errors during training does not force the model to learn a more accurate temporal structure; it merely permits a shifted but otherwise degraded output.

\section{Objective 2: Multi-Step Forecasting} \label{sec:exp:obj2}
% ================================================================

Multi-step supervision~\citep{fox2018multioutput} replaces the single output at $t+H$ with 12 simultaneous predictions at $t+1, \ldots, t+H$, as described in Section~\ref{sec:method:obj2}. The MSE loss is computed uniformly across all 12 predicted steps. Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:obj2} and Fig.~\ref{fig:exp:multistep_plot} report the results.

Multi-step supervision achieves the best trajectory accuracy among all evaluated strategies. The LSTM multi-step model reaches an RMSE of 35.59~mg/dL and an MAE of 26.80~mg/dL, the lowest values observed across the full set of ten configurations. The PatchTST multi-step model achieves its best RMSE (39.05~mg/dL) and MAE (28.84~mg/dL) among all PatchTST variants. The reduction in prediction error is modest but consistent across both architectures, which is explained by the broader supervision signal: computing the loss over all 12 output steps reduces the weight of any single large error at the endpoint and encourages the model to produce a more accurate overall trajectory. For LSTM, the lag-adjusted RMSE (RMSE$^*$) improves from 21.65~mg/dL (MSE baseline) to 20.74~mg/dL with multi-step supervision, indicating that the broader supervision signal marginally improves trajectory quality once the temporal displacement is corrected. For PatchTST, the relationship reverses: the MSE baseline achieves RMSE$^*$ = 14.21~mg/dL, slightly lower than the multi-step variant at 14.53~mg/dL, indicating that multi-step training does not consistently improve lag-adjusted accuracy for the attention-based architecture.

As a result, the predicted threshold crossing remains systematically delayed, and the improvement in detection performance is not clinically meaningful on its own.

\section{Objective 3: Event-Centric Evaluation} \label{sec:exp:obj3}
% ================================================================

This section evaluates hypoglycaemia detection from two complementary perspectives. The tolerance sweep quantifies how much of the detection failure of forecast-derived detectors is attributable to timing mismatch rather than structural inability to anticipate the event. The direct classification analysis then tests whether a task-specific training objective resolves the remaining gap.

\subsection{Forecast-Derived Detection: Tolerance Sweep}

The tolerance sweep evaluates how forecast-derived event detection changes as the acceptance window $\tau$ is widened from 0 to 60~minutes in 5-minute steps (Section~\ref{sec:bg:metrics}). Full numerical results are provided in Tabs.~\ref{tab:app:tau_sweep_lstm} and~\ref{tab:app:tau_sweep_patchtst} in Appendix~\ref{appendix:detailed_metrics}. Fig.~\ref{fig:exp:tau_sweep_plot} summarises the F1 curves; Figs.~\ref{fig:exp:tau_sweep_lstm} and~\ref{fig:exp:tau_sweep_patchtst} show per-metric breakdowns for all training strategies.

The sweep reveals a fundamental architectural difference. For PatchTST, the F1 score rises steeply as $\tau$ increases: the MSE baseline rises from 0.061 at $\tau = 15$~min to 0.551 at $\tau = 60$~min. For LSTM, the same strategy rises only from 0.005 to 0.113 over the same range. This contrast indicates that PatchTST produces predicted threshold crossings that are structurally present but temporally displaced, while the LSTM rarely produces threshold crossings at all, regardless of how wide the tolerance window becomes. Offset-aware training strategies do not resolve this pattern: even the Soft-DTW LSTM, which achieves the largest lag reduction, reaches only F1 = 0.082 at $\tau = 60$~min, compared to 0.551 for PatchTST MSE.

\subsection{Direct Binary Event Classifiers}
\label{sec:exp:obj3:classifiers}

Direct binary classifiers~\citep{shao2024hypoglycemia, cinar2025hypoclassification} are trained end-to-end on the event label defined in Equation~\ref{eq:eventlabel}, using a weighted binary cross-entropy loss to address the class imbalance (Section~\ref{sec:method:setup:cgm}). Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:classifiers} and Fig.~\ref{fig:exp:classifier_traces} report the results.

Both classifiers substantially outperform the best forecast-derived detectors. The LSTM event classifier achieves a recall of 0.693 and an F2 of 0.463, compared to a best forecast-derived F2 of 0.013 for the LSTM under Bounded-Lag loss. The PatchTST event classifier achieves a recall of 0.615 and an F2 of 0.178, compared to a best forecast-derived F2 of 0.085. The large gains in recall indicate that a task-specific training objective is far more effective at detecting hypoglycaemia than threshold-based detection on a regression output. Both improvements are statistically significant after Holm--Bonferroni correction: for LSTM, median $\Delta F2 = 0.460$ [IQR 0.319--0.575], $r = 1.00$, $p = 5.3 \times 10^{-7}$ (Test~1); for PatchTST, median $\Delta F2 = 0.108$ [IQR 0.001--0.199], $r = 0.60$, $p = 1.5 \times 10^{-3}$ (Test~2); see Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:significance}.

A performance inversion is observed between the two architectures. In the forecasting tasks, the LSTM consistently achieves lower RMSE (36.09~mg/dL) than PatchTST (39.27~mg/dL). In the classification task, this ordering reverses: the LSTM event classifier achieves F2~=~0.463, substantially higher than the PatchTST classifier at F2~=~0.178 (median $\Delta F2 = 0.288$ [IQR 0.206--0.346], $r = 1.00$, $p = 5.3 \times 10^{-7}$; Test~3, Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:significance}). This inversion is explained by the Reversible Instance Normalisation (RevIN)~\citep{kim2022revin} used in PatchTST (Section~\ref{sec:bg:models:patchtst}): a classifier operating on its scale-free inputs cannot reliably associate a window with the absolute 70~mg/dL threshold required to trigger a positive label, whereas the LSTM processes the raw glucose values and can directly learn proximity to the clinical threshold.

\subsection{Lead-Time Analysis}
\label{sec:exp:obj3:leadtime}

Fig.~\ref{fig:exp:lead_time} shows the distribution of lead times for all true-positive detections across the 36-patient cohort. The lead time is defined as the elapsed time from alarm issuance to the first hypoglycaemic step in the prediction window. The LSTM classifier has a median lead time of 5~minutes and a mean of 12.4~minutes. Of its 2{,}610 true positives, 1{,}293 (49.5\%) fire at lead time zero, meaning the alarm is issued at the moment when the patient has already crossed the 70~mg/dL threshold. The PatchTST classifier has a median lead time of 15~minutes and a mean of 18.3~minutes. Of its 2{,}243 true positives, 802 (35.8\%) are reactive, with the remainder distributed more evenly across the 5-to-55 minute range.

These results indicate that the LSTM classifier responds largely to an ongoing glucose decline rather than anticipating a future one. Despite achieving higher F2, the majority of its true-positive alarms provide little or no advance warning. The PatchTST classifier, while weaker in overall detection performance, produces a higher proportion of genuine advance warnings. This distinction is relevant for clinical use, where a warning issued after the event has already begun is of limited practical value.

Taken together, the lead-time distribution qualifies the interpretation of the F2 results in Section~\ref{sec:exp:obj3:classifiers}. The high recall of the LSTM classifier is driven to a substantial degree by detections that coincide with the onset of hypoglycaemia rather than precede it: with 49.5\% of true positives issued at zero lead time, close to half of the LSTM alarms confirm an ongoing event instead of anticipating a future one. A high F2 score therefore does not by itself establish that the classifier has learned to anticipate hypoglycaemia, as it partly reflects the detection of glucose values that are already low at the moment of the alarm. The PatchTST classifier exhibits the opposite profile, trading lower overall detection performance for a larger share of genuinely anticipatory alarms. Detection performance and anticipatory capability are thus distinct properties that F2 alone does not jointly capture, a distinction that must be accounted for when reading the classifier results as evidence of predictive rather than reactive behaviour.

\section{Overall Comparison} \label{sec:exp:overall}
% ================================================================

Across all ten configurations (Tabs.~\ref{tab:exp:baseline}--\ref{tab:exp:classifiers}), no single configuration dominates both forecasting quality and event detection performance.

Among the eight forecast-derived configurations, LSTM multi-step achieves the best trajectory accuracy with an RMSE of 35.59~mg/dL and an MAE of 26.80~mg/dL. PatchTST multi-step achieves the highest event detection F2 among forecast-derived configurations at 0.085. However, all forecast-derived F2 scores remain below 0.09, which indicates that threshold-based detection on a regression output is fundamentally limited regardless of the training strategy.

The direct event classifiers stand apart from the forecasting configurations. The LSTM event classifier achieves an F2 of 0.463 and a recall of 0.693, which is more than five times the best forecast-derived F2 value (PatchTST Multi-step: F2~=~0.085); classifier superiority over forecast-derived detection is confirmed statistically for both architectures (Tests~1--2; Tab.~\ref{tab:exp:significance}). This statistical superiority concerns the recall-weighted F2 criterion; as the lead-time analysis in Section~\ref{sec:exp:obj3:leadtime} shows, a substantial fraction of the LSTM classifier's true positives are reactive rather than anticipatory, so the gain in F2 should not be read as evidence of reliable advance warning. This indicates that a task-specific training objective addresses the detection failure far more effectively than any modification to the forecasting loss or output structure.

The best trajectory quality and the best event detection performance are achieved by different model configurations. LSTM multi-step leads on RMSE and MAE, while LSTM direct classifier leads on recall, F1, and F2. These two strengths do not overlap in a single configuration, which indicates that trajectory forecasting and event detection measure fundamentally different aspects of model behaviour. A practical system that requires both a high-quality glucose forecast and hypoglycaemia detection support would need to deploy the two best-performing models separately, with each used for the task it is optimised for; the limited lead time and high false-alarm rate of the classifier (Sections~\ref{sec:exp:obj3:leadtime} and~\ref{sec:conc:limitations}) constrain the degree to which it can serve as an anticipatory warning system.
